Summary:
This paper systematically studies the design space of LLM-based social simulations by running 595 simulation roll-outs across seven parameters, including population size, base model choice, network topology, homophily, survey context, news-agent presence, and population proportions. Using surveyed opinions as a proxy for agent beliefs, the authors find that base model choice is the most influential factor, that social-media fine-tuning reduces AI detectability and intensifies opinion dynamics in their ablation setting, and that parameter interactions are only partially additive, making simulation outcomes difficult to predict from individual design choices alone.
Reasons To Accept:
S1. LLM-based social simulation is a widely studied research direction. The authors correctly identify the lack of validation standards in this field as an important and urgent problem.
S2. Treating design choices as objects of study has practical value, as it helps diagnose whether observed simulation outcomes are robust to design choices or highly dependent on modeling decisions.
S3. The experiment is well designed. The analysis of parameter interactions yields the useful conclusion that the design space exhibits mixed interaction effects, meaning that individual parameter impacts cannot be studied in isolation and linearly combined. The fine-tuning ablation demonstrates substantial differences in opinion dynamics before and after fine-tuning. These findings have practical implications for designing simulation systems.
Reasons To Reject:
W1. The paper is motivated by validating model realism, but the evidence mostly supports internal sensitivity analysis rather than realism validation. Although the authors acknowledge the lack of human baselines, this is not fully integrated into the paper's broader claims about validation, realism, and design quality. As a result, the study can show which design choices change internal metrics, but cannot determine which designs are better or more realistic.
W2. The role of the base model is under-interpreted. The paper treats base model as a single experimental variable, but model identity bundles together parameter scale, instruction tuning, alignment behavior, etc. The authors acknowledge confounding with question-specific initial consensus, but do not discuss these broader sources of confounding. Therefore, the finding that "base model is the most important variable" is descriptively useful but has limited explanatory power.
W3. Section 3.2 motivates an important contrast between Average and Distribution, namely whether matching the full distribution of human opinions matters more than matching only the modal answer. However, the results section does not substantially return to this question. The fact that Distribution and Average obtain very similar SimBench scores despite different weight distributions further deserves analysis.
W4. The scope of applicability is not well specified. The paper defines Silicon Societies broadly, covering both controlled simulators and, to a lesser extent, naturally emerging LLM-populated social networks such as Moltbook. These systems differ substantially in researcher control, agent heterogeneity, environmental openness, and validation signals. Since the experiments are conducted in a controlled simulator, it remains unclear how far the conclusions transfer to broader Silicon Societies.

Comment: We thank the reviewer for the careful and constructive review, and for recognizing the importance of the validation gap, the practical value of treating design choices as objects of study, and the contribution of the parameter-interaction analysis and fine-tuning ablation. We address each weakness below.

W1. Internal sensitivity vs. realism validation.
This is a fair limitation. Ground-truth matched human-trajectory data at the scale required for this kind of validation does not currently exist, and the field has not converged on strong operational realism metrics (Larooij and Tornberg, 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). The intended contribution of the paper is to characterize the design space, that is, to surface which axes change simulator outcomes and how predictably, rather than to rank designs by realism. We will revise to make this scope explicit: realism-related language will be confined to the stylistic-classifier results (the one component grounded in human reference data), and the behavioural and structural metrics will be reframed as internal-sensitivity findings rather than evidence of human-likeness. We will also expand the discussion of the data constraints (privacy-preserving aggregation in available datasets, absence of matched human-trajectory benchmarks) that currently limit broader external validation, framing it as an open problem for the field rather than a contribution this paper claims to make.

W2. The role of the base model is under-interpreted.
The reviewer is correct that the "base model" bundles many factors such as parameter scale and architectural choices amongst others. We will expand both the limitations and future-work sections to enumerate these confounds explicitly, and add discussion to the model-importance results so that the finding is read as descriptive rather than causal-explanatory.

The reason model identity is treated monolithically in this paper is that the existing simulation literature treats it as a largely neutral implementation detail. Our central methodological claim is that this neutrality assumption is empirically wrong, and consequently that simulation outcomes cannot be safely interpreted without controlling for the base model. Disentangling the model-internal contributors to that effect is a substantial follow-up programme in its own right, falling within the (necessarily long) list of additional parameters that further work should test. We will explicitly mark this as required future work and flag where in the paper this caveat should temper interpretation.

W3. Average vs. Distribution underexplored.
We thank the reviewer for surfacing this contrast, and will add a dedicated subsection on it. The headline result is that the clean "average causes the strongest consensus erosion" finding from the single-model setting does not survive once the base model is varied. On the full multi-model dataset, average vs. uniform is no longer significant as a main effect (p = 0.29, ns), and the effect resurfaces as a model x proportions interaction:

The average scheme (optimized convex combination of archetype weights) produces the strongest erosion only for Minitaur, while distribution dominates for Llama-3.1 and Qwen, and blueprint produces mild or positive drift for the non-Minitaur models. We interpret this carefully: proportions appear to matter, but the direction of the effect on consensus depends on the base model, with no single scheme consistently dominating. We read this as an additional and unusually clean example of the non-additive interaction regime described elsewhere in the paper: a parameter that looks meaningful in isolation under one model behaves differently or even oppositely under another. Substantively, this suggests that population-composition choices cannot be evaluated independently of the base model in use, and that prior work fixing one of them risks reporting an effect that does not generalize. We will fold this analysis into the results section.

W4. Scope of "Silicon Societies".
We agree, and will rescope all empirical conclusions to controlled simulators of the kind studied here, and explicitly note that naturally emerging LLM-populated networks (e.g., Moltbook-like platforms) differ substantially in researcher control, environmental openness, and validation signals. The introductory framing will be tightened so that the broader "Silicon Society" definition is used only to motivate the problem, while the empirical claims are tagged to controlled-simulator settings.
