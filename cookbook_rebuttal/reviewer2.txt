Summary:
This paper studies the design spaces of LLM-driven social simulations, to which the authors call "Silicon Society". The paper alters several design choices, including the number of agents, base model used, etc, and study how they affect on the evaluation metrics proposed.
My recommendation is to marginally reject (5) or reject (4) this paper, mainly because:
The paper is framed to study the design space, so I was expecting to see a balanced discussion of different choices. However, the paper spends much time discussing the model choices, and did not discuss factors like network topology, which the authors claimed to in the variable sections. There's no serious analysis of topology in the paper, only mentioned the settings tested in the appendix. It just made me feel that the paper overemphasize and analyze the effects of models.
About the realism claim: I am not sure too what extent the experiments with BERT classifier validate the claims about realism, because it is trained on BluePrint domain, and then test the models finetuned on the BluePrint domain, which makes me think it's more like in-domain detector. Moreover, even after finetuning, the accuracy is like ~95% at best, which means the AI generated texts are basically always distinguishable and not realistic.
I feel that there are many design choices that are apparent and interesting but the paper didn't test on - for instance, there are simulation works that have more components in the simulation, such as modeling the agents' daily life, having opinion leaders, etc - the paper could spend less time talking about models and more about these nontrivial simulation components.

Comment: We thank the reviewer for engaging substantively with the work, and for recognizing the topic's relevance to the social simulation community and the soundness of the experimental setup. We address the three weaknesses below.

W1. Imbalance between model and topology analysis;
Topology occupies less space than model because, in the broad-exploration design underlying our headline finding, between-model variance dominates and masks the topology signal. In the broad sweep across two topologies and other factors, every behavioural-metric main effect of topology is small and non-significant (e.g., Net Consensus Change: random vs. power-law $\Delta = -0.014$, p = 0.230, d = -0.10). This supports one of the paper's methodological warnings: a null topology result in a heterogeneous design is consistent with a masked effect, not an absent one.
To test this directly, we conducted an additional controlled study that fixes other variables and varies only the followership graph across seven topologies: ER,Barabasi-Albert, Stochastic Block Model, cycle, power-law cluster, fully connected, and Forest Fire. Under this controlled design, the topology signal is large and reproducible:
Neighbour Alignment Shift Rate (NASR): $\eta^2 = 0.906$ ($p_\text{perm} < 0.001$).
Net Consensus Change: $\eta^2 = 0.285$ ($p_\text{perm} < 0.001$).
NASR ranges from 0.068 (Barabasi-Albert) to 0.121 (cycle), a 77% relative difference.
The NASR effect survives Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction across three additional LLM families (Gemma-3-4B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-7B; q < 0.01 each), three survey questions, the presence or absence of the news agent, and density-matched graph parameterizations. Among the five parameterizable topologies matched to approximately 16 mean degree, graph density does not predict NASR (r = 0.063, q = 0.364), indicating that the effect is structural rather than density-driven.
We kept this study out of the main paper because topology merits first-class treatment, and a full analysis requires more space than the present submission permits. In the revision, we will expand the topology discussion and report a headline summary of this controlled result, positioning topology accurately as a non-neutral design choice rather than the “random init” default often assumed in the literature.
The current emphasis on model is also empirically motivated: in the broad sweep, base model overwhelms every other factor, including topology. Surfacing this to a literature that often treats model identity as a neutral implementation detail is one of the paper's central contributions. We will revise the framing to present this as a finding that motivates controlled designs, such as the topology study above, rather than as an a priori emphasis.

W2. BERT classifier is in-domain.
We agree and will tighten the realism language throughout. Specifically, we will (i) describe the BERT score as a stylistic-realism metric, (ii) state explicitly that the classifier is trained and tested in-domain, and (iii) avoid implying that it establishes behavioural or sociological realism. We will also evaluate the classifier on text from a different LLM-based simulator and compare it against at least one off-the-shelf AI-detection tool, reporting agreement and failure cases.
Regarding the 95% figure, a high-capacity domain-specific classifier provides an upper bound on machine detectability, not a lower bound on human-perceived realism. In a separate study using these fine-tuned models, human raters presented with real and AI-generated tweet pairs identified our models' tweets as AI approximately 56% of the time, compared with chance performance of 50% and more than 70% for off-the-shelf base models. We deliberately did not use this Turing-style result as our realism claim: humans are weak LLM detectors, so doing so would overstate realism. The BERT classifier is the more conservative benchmark. We will discuss this trade-off explicitly and clarify that the 95% figure reflects the classifier's discriminative power on in-domain data more than a generation-quality failure.

W3.Design choices left untested
We acknowledge this limitation. Given the page limit, we chose to study a small number of foundational axes carefully rather than many axes shallowly. The mechanisms the reviewer suggests are important directions for follow-up work, but cannot be cleanly studied until the effects of model, network, population composition, and survey context are characterized.
The topology follow-up illustrates why: topology appears inert in the heterogeneous design (effect approximately 0, p ≥ 0.2) but becomes highly consequential once base-model and other confounds are fixed ($\eta^2 = 0.906$). Studying more sophisticated mechanisms without first establishing these foundational confounds risks attributing base-model artifacts to other factors. The axes studied here may appear basic, but they are load-bearing. We will make this scoping rationale explicit in the revision.
